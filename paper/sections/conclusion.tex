\section{Conclusion}

LGFV exit reform is not only a debt management policy. It is a test of whether
the central state can formalize local fiscal behavior after years of reliance on
quasi-fiscal instruments. This paper argues that the meaning of exit varies
across localities. Some cities achieve substantive exit, while others display
nominal compliance or transfer financing functions to new entities.

By disaggregating LGFV exit into institutional types, the paper shifts attention
from whether localities exited to what kind of exit they achieved. This
distinction provides a sharper way to study center-local relations, fiscal
formalization, and historical persistence in contemporary Chinese political
economy.

The next empirical step is therefore not simply to add more observations. It is
to preserve the institutional distinction that motivates the paper. Two tasks
are central. First, the coding procedure must expand from pilot cases to a
city-platform sample while keeping candidate cases separate from final
human-validated labels. Second, the historical capacity measures must be linked
to these exit types in a design that distinguishes substantive exit from nominal
exit, functional transfer, and liquidation. If the argument is correct,
historically stronger localities should be better able to move former platform
functions into formal fiscal and budgetary channels, while weaker localities
should be more likely to preserve those functions through renamed platforms,
restructured obligations, or continued fiscal support.
