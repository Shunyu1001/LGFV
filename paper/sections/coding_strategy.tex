\section{Coding Strategy}

The previous section defined the case-level research design. This section
describes how the main outcome is measured. The coding strategy begins from a
simple premise: official exit language is evidence about regulatory status, not
by itself evidence that a government financing function disappeared. For each
platform, I therefore code both the formal event and the post-event location of
the relevant function.

\subsection{Source hierarchy}

The source hierarchy begins with government documents, finance-bureau notices,
state-owned asset supervision documents, exchange disclosures, and company
announcements when they are available. These sources are most useful for
identifying official exit, market-oriented transformation, asset transfer, debt
replacement, restructuring, and liquidation. Bond prospectuses, credit rating
reports, legal opinions, annual reports, and audited financial statements are
often more informative about continuing public-project functions, repayment
arrangements, fiscal support, issuer guarantees, related-party transfers, and
changes in the role of the company after exit. Credible financial news and
company profiles serve as supplementary sources. These materials can help
locate events and identify entities, but they do not by themselves determine
the final label.

This hierarchy matters because the most authoritative source for the formal
event is not always the most informative source for the post-event function. A
government notice may state that a platform exited a list, while a later
prospectus may show that the same issuer still carries out infrastructure
investment, entrusted construction, land preparation, or project repayment on
behalf of the local state. Conversely, a prospectus may contain
no-government-financing language, while the underlying fiscal-substitution
arrangement appears only in a debt-replacement announcement or a municipal
budget document. The coding rule therefore treats each source as evidence for
a specific component of the case rather than as a global statement about the
whole outcome.

\subsection{Coding rule}

The coding decision begins by identifying the baseline platform function. The
relevant evidence includes infrastructure investment and financing, land
development, construction entrusted by government, public utility operation,
policy-driven housing or urban renewal, and repayment arrangements tied to
local fiscal resources. I then identify the formal event, which may be exit
from a financing-platform list, a no-government-financing statement,
market-oriented transformation, merger, asset transfer, debt replacement, debt
restructuring, or dissolution. The final step before assigning a label is to
locate the post-event function. The same company may continue the function, the
function may move into the budget, the function may be assigned to another
local state-owned entity, or the original function may terminate. The exit type
is assigned only after these elements are jointly evaluated under the fixed
codebook.

Substantive exit requires evidence that the platform no longer performs
government financing or quasi-fiscal financing functions after the relevant
event. This label does not require the company to stop all public-sector
activity. A company may still manage public projects if the financing has moved
inside formal fiscal channels and the issuer receives management fees rather
than using its balance sheet as an off-budget financing device. Nominal exit
requires formal exit or no-government-financing language combined with evidence
that the same platform continues the relevant government financing function.
Functional transfer requires evidence that the function moved to another
state-owned entity, merged group, subsidiary, or newly established platform.
Liquidation requires evidence of dissolution, deregistration, bankruptcy, or
termination.

The main ambiguity concerns the boundary between substantive exit and
functional transfer. Both can involve a decline in the old platform's financing
role. The difference lies in where the function goes. If the function is absorbed
into formal fiscal instruments, budgetary management, or fee-based project
management without a new platform taking over the quasi-fiscal role, the case
is closer to substantive exit. If the function is reassigned to another
state-owned entity or consolidated into a new platform group, the case is
closer to functional transfer. A second ambiguity concerns nominal exit. Legal
language stating that new debt does not constitute local government debt is not
sufficient for substantive exit if the same document shows continuing public
project financing, fiscal repayment, or government-entrusted construction.

These cases illustrate distinctions that are central to the measurement
strategy. Formal compliance language must be separated from functional
evidence. A prospectus may state that new debt does not constitute local
government debt, while other sections describe continuing public project
obligations. Chengdu makes this distinction especially clear because the
no-government-financing statement appears in the same document as evidence of
self-funded and financed infrastructure construction with fiscal payment
arrangements. Continuing public project management is also not always evidence
of nominal exit. In Guangzhou, the relevant distinction is that government
projects were funded through municipal fiscal funds and the company received a
management fee, rather than using the platform as an off-budget financing arm.
Functional transfer, finally, is not merely a name change. In Hangzhou, the
classification rests on equity transfer, debt succession, and the creation of a
specialized housing-security group within a reorganized municipal platform
system.

\subsection{LLM-assisted labeling}

Large language models are used to produce preliminary labels from the same
codebook and source excerpts. They do not assign final labels. The purpose of
using LLMs is to make the source review more scalable and to force each case to
be evaluated under a common decision rule. The model prompt asks for a proposed
label, a confidence level, a short rationale, the exact pieces of evidence used
for the classification, and the most plausible alternative label. The prompt
also requires the model to return \texttt{unclear} when the source packet lacks
a formal event or lacks evidence about the post-event function.

Human validation compares the model's evidence claims with the original
documents and removes claims that are not supported by the source text. It also
evaluates whether the model has confused legal compliance language with
functional change. This error is especially likely in bond prospectuses because
issuers often state that new debt is not local government debt while also
describing public-project obligations and fiscal payment arrangements. The
human-validated label file records the final label, alternative label,
confidence level, source coverage score, key evidence, and remaining caveat.
The final dataset is therefore not a set of model outputs. It is a set of
human-reviewed case labels whose preliminary coding was assisted by LLMs.

The audit trail is part of the measurement contribution. Each final label is
linked to a case identifier, document identifiers, source types, line
references where available, model rationales, human rationale, alternative
label, ambiguity note, and source coverage score. This structure makes it
possible to revise a label when new documents are found without changing the
codebook. It also makes disagreement informative. When an LLM proposes
substantive exit because a prospectus contains compliance language, while human
review identifies continuing government-entrusted construction in the same
document, the disagreement identifies exactly the distinction the paper seeks
to measure.

Candidate cases are kept separate from final labels. Luzhou Xinglu is a useful
near-miss case. The source packet strongly rejects a substantive-exit
interpretation: it shows infrastructure investment-financing,
government-entrusted construction, fiscal receivables, project repayment, and
government support across 2015, 2017, 2018, 2021, and 2023. The case
nevertheless remains outside the final label table because the project has not
found a direct platform-list exit source. Zunyi Daoqiao is treated similarly.
Its documents strongly indicate continuing platform status and debt-resolution
pressure, including a rating notice that places the company inside Guizhou's
government-financing-platform debt-resolution pilot, but the case remains
outside the final label table until a direct formal-exit or
no-government-financing source is collected.
