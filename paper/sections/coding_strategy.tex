\section{Coding Strategy}

The previous section defined the case-level research design. This section
describes how the main outcome is measured. The coding strategy begins from a
simple premise: official exit language is evidence about regulatory status, not
by itself evidence about the disappearance of a government financing function.
For each platform, I therefore code both the formal exit event and the
post-exit location of the relevant function.

The source hierarchy has three levels. First, I use government documents,
finance-bureau notices, state-owned asset supervision documents, and company
announcements when they are available. These sources are most useful for
identifying official exit, restructuring, asset transfers, debt replacement, or
liquidation. Second, I use bond prospectuses, credit rating reports, legal
opinions, and annual reports. These sources are often more informative about
continuing project functions, repayment arrangements, fiscal support, and
changes in the issuer's role. Third, I use credible financial news and company
profiles as supplementary sources. These materials can help locate events and
entities, but they do not by themselves determine the final label.

The coding decision has three steps. First, I identify the relevant official
event: exit from a platform list, market-oriented transformation,
reorganization, merger, debt replacement, or dissolution. Second, I identify
the post-event location of the function. This requires evidence on whether the
same company still finances or implements public projects, whether the function
has been absorbed by budgetary instruments, whether another local SOE has
assumed the function, or whether the original entity has ceased operations.
Third, I assign the exit type using the fixed codebook. Substantive exit
requires evidence that the platform no longer performs government financing or
quasi-fiscal functions. Nominal exit requires formal exit language combined
with continuing functional evidence in the same platform. Functional transfer
requires evidence that the function moved to another state entity, merged
group, subsidiary, or newly established platform. Liquidation requires evidence
of dissolution, deregistration, bankruptcy, or termination.

These cases illustrate three distinctions that are central to the measurement
strategy. First, formal compliance language must be separated from functional
evidence. A prospectus may state that new debt does not constitute local
government debt, while other sections describe continuing public project
obligations. Chengdu makes this distinction especially clear because the
no-government-financing statement appears in the same document as evidence of
self-funded and financed infrastructure construction with fiscal payment
arrangements. Second, continuing public project management is not always
evidence of nominal exit. In Guangzhou, the relevant distinction is that
government projects were funded through municipal fiscal funds and the company
received a management fee, rather than using the platform as an off-budget
financing arm. Third, functional transfer is not merely a name change. In
Hangzhou, the classification rests on equity transfer, debt succession, and the
creation of a specialized housing-security group within a reorganized municipal
platform system.

The workflow is LLM-assisted and human-validated. Large language models are
used to produce preliminary labels from the same codebook and source excerpts.
They must return a label, a confidence level, a rationale, and the specific
textual evidence for the classification. The final label is assigned only after
human review of the original documents. This procedure has two advantages.
First, it makes the coding process more scalable once the source inventory
expands beyond the pilot cases. Second, it preserves an audit trail linking
each final label to document identifiers, line references, confidence levels,
alternative plausible labels, and remaining caveats.
