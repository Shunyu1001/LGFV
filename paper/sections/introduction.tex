\section{Introduction}

China's local government financing vehicles (LGFVs) have long occupied a
central position in local fiscal governance. They allowed local governments to
finance infrastructure, land development, industrial parks, urban construction,
and other quasi-public projects outside the ordinary budget
\citep{oi1992,oi1995,wong2013,tsui2011}. This organizational form helped local
governments manage a persistent tension in China's fiscal system: local
governments carry extensive expenditure responsibilities, but they have faced
tight formal constraints on ordinary borrowing \citep{montinola1995,jin2005}.
When land transfer revenues were high and urban expansion generated large
expected returns, LGFVs appeared to reconcile these pressures by linking land
development, infrastructure investment, and local debt financing. When land
revenues declined and repayment risks increased, the same arrangement became a
central object of fiscal and financial regulation \citep{tsui2011,bai2016longshadow}.

The central government has responded by requiring LGFVs to withdraw from
government financing functions. Recent official reports describe large
reductions in the number of platform companies and the volume of their
operational financial debt. These figures suggest substantial progress, but
they leave open a more basic institutional question. When a platform exits,
what happens to the function it previously performed? A company may be removed
from an official list, renamed, merged into a state capital operation company,
or declared to have completed market-oriented transformation. Yet the financing,
construction, project-management, and debt functions attached to that company
may disappear, remain inside the same issuer, move to another local
state-owned entity, or become absorbed into formal fiscal instruments. The
problem is therefore not only whether LGFV exit has occurred. It is what kind
of exit has occurred.

This paper studies the institutional types of LGFV exit. By \emph{exit type}, I
mean the relationship between an official exit event and the post-event
location of the government financing function. I distinguish four types. First,
\emph{substantive exit} occurs when a platform company genuinely ceases to
perform government financing or quasi-fiscal financing functions. Second,
\emph{nominal exit} occurs when official exit or no-government-financing
language appears, but the same platform continues to perform similar functions.
Third, \emph{functional transfer} occurs when the original platform exits while
its financing, construction, or debt-management function moves to another local
state-owned or quasi-state entity. Fourth, liquidation occurs when the company
is dissolved, deregistered, bankrupted, or otherwise terminates its operations.
This classification shifts the analysis from official exit as a binary event
to the institutional content of exit.

The main argument is that historically rooted local state capacity shapes which
type of exit a locality can achieve. LGFV exit is not simply a regulatory
instruction to remove a label. It requires local governments to replace the
functions previously performed by platforms through formal fiscal instruments,
budgetary channels, regulated state assets, or genuinely commercial operations.
This process requires three capacities. First, local officials must classify
platform projects and liabilities into public, commercial, and nonviable
obligations. Second, they must move eligible public investment into
special-purpose bonds, budgetary spending, or regulated state asset
arrangements. Third, they must monitor whether the post-exit firm or a related
state-owned entity recreates implicit government financing. Localities with
stronger historically rooted administrative and fiscal capacity should be more
able to make this substitution. In these localities, official exit is more
likely to become substantive exit. Localities with weaker capacity should be
more likely to preserve the old function through nominal compliance or
functional transfer.

The argument links a contemporary debt-management reform to a longer problem in
Chinese state building. Central authorities have repeatedly attempted to
standardize and supervise local fiscal behavior, while local officials have
needed flexible resources to provide public goods, maintain growth, and respond
to administrative mandates. LGFVs are a contemporary expression of this
tension. They converted local development obligations into organizational and
financial arrangements that stood near the boundary of the formal budget. The
exit reform is therefore not only a campaign to reduce hidden debt. It is also
an attempt to move local fiscal activity from quasi-formal instruments into
more visible, budgetary, and supervisable institutions. The success of that
attempt depends on the administrative capacity of the local state.

Empirically, the paper develops a new case-level coding strategy. The unit of
analysis is a city-platform case: a major LGFV or platform-like local
state-owned enterprise located within a prefecture-level city. For each case, I
code the formal exit event and the post-event location of the relevant function.
The evidence comes from government announcements, finance-bureau and
state-owned asset supervision documents, company disclosures, bond
prospectuses, credit rating reports, legal opinions, annual reports, business
registration records, and credible financial news. Large language models are
used to generate preliminary labels under a fixed codebook, but final labels
are assigned only after human review of the original documents. The current
pilot contains five human-validated cases and several candidate cases that
remain outside the final label file until a formal event is documented. The
validated cases already recover three distinct institutional outcomes:
substantive exit in Guangzhou, functional transfer in Hangzhou, and nominal
exit in Xi'an, Chengdu, and Zhuzhou.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it offers a measurement
contribution to the study of local debt reform by disaggregating official LGFV
exit into substantive exit, nominal exit, functional transfer, and liquidation.
This distinction matters because aggregate reductions in platform counts or
reported debt can combine very different institutional outcomes. Second, it
develops a theoretical argument about formal fiscal substitution. Local
compliance with central fiscal formalization depends not only on contemporary
economic conditions or debt pressure, but also on historically rooted
administrative and fiscal capacity. Third, it introduces an auditable
LLM-assisted, human-validated coding procedure for studying organizational
change in settings where the key outcome is embedded in heterogeneous
administrative, financial, and corporate documents.
