\section{Introduction}

China's local government financing vehicle (LGFV) exit reform poses a simple
but consequential measurement problem. Official reports describe large
reductions in the number of platform companies and the volume of their
operational financial debt. These figures suggest progress in local debt
management, but they do not show what happened to the government financing
function that platform companies previously performed. This paper argues that
official exit is not institutional exit. Historical state capacity shapes
whether exit becomes substantive, nominal, or transferred.

The distinction matters because LGFVs have long been central to local fiscal
governance. They allowed local governments to finance infrastructure, land
development, industrial parks, urban construction, and other quasi-public
projects outside the ordinary budget \citep{oi1992,oi1995,wong2013,tsui2011}.
They also helped local governments manage a persistent fiscal tension: local
governments carry extensive expenditure responsibilities, but they have faced
tight formal constraints on ordinary borrowing \citep{montinola1995,jin2005}.
When land revenues declined and repayment risks increased, the same
organizational form became a central object of fiscal and financial regulation
\citep{tsui2011,bai2016longshadow}.

This paper studies the institutional types of LGFV exit. By \emph{exit type}, I
mean the relationship between an official exit event and the post-event
location of the government financing function. A platform may achieve
\emph{substantive exit} by genuinely ceasing to perform government financing or
quasi-fiscal financing functions. It may display \emph{nominal exit} when
official exit or no-government-financing language appears while the same
platform continues to perform similar functions. It may undergo
\emph{functional transfer} when the original platform exits but its financing,
construction, or debt-management function moves to another local state-owned or
quasi-state entity. In other cases, the firm may disappear through liquidation,
deregistration, bankruptcy, or another form of termination. This classification
shifts the analysis from official exit as a binary event to the institutional
content of exit.

The argument is that historically rooted local state capacity shapes which type
of exit a locality can achieve. LGFV exit is not simply a regulatory
instruction to remove a label. It requires local governments to replace the
functions previously performed by platforms through formal fiscal instruments,
budgetary channels, regulated state assets, or genuinely commercial operations.
That substitution depends on fiscal absorption, bureaucratic coordination, and
project and state-asset governance. These capacities allow local officials to
classify platform obligations, move eligible public investment into regulated
fiscal channels, coordinate asset and debt reorganization, and monitor whether
post-exit firms recreate hidden debt through entrusted construction, repurchase
agreements, subsidiaries, or fiscal subsidies. Compliance documents may reveal
these capacities, but they are not the mechanism itself.

Localities with stronger historically rooted administrative and fiscal capacity
should be more able to make this substitution. In these localities, official
exit is more likely to become substantive exit, or at least a regulated
reorganization of former platform functions. Localities with weaker capacity
should be more likely to preserve the old function through nominal compliance
or functional transfer. The argument therefore links a contemporary
debt-management reform to a longer problem in Chinese state building: whether
local fiscal activity can be moved from quasi-formal organizational devices
into visible, budgetary, and supervisable institutions.

Empirically, the paper develops a case-level coding strategy. The unit of
analysis is a city-platform case, and each label records both the formal exit
event and the post-event location of the relevant function. The evidence comes
from government announcements, finance-bureau and state-owned asset supervision
documents, company disclosures, bond prospectuses, credit rating reports, legal
opinions, annual reports, business registration records, and credible financial
news. Large language models generate preliminary labels under a fixed codebook,
but final labels are assigned only after human review of the original
documents.

The current pilot contains twenty evidence packets: twelve human-validated
labels, four strong candidates, one boundary case, and three source-only
candidates. The validated labels already recover three distinct institutional
outcomes. Guangzhou and Ningbo are coded as substantive exit. Hangzhou,
Wenzhou, and Foshan are coded as functional transfer. Xi'an, Chengdu, Zhuzhou,
Xuzhou, Hengyang, Jinan, and Yancheng are coded as nominal exit. A small
descriptive comparison links these labels to historical-capacity bins: the
validated low-capacity cases are all nominal exits, while the high-capacity
cases include substantive exit, functional transfer, and nominal exit. This is
not yet a population estimate, but it shows that the coding scheme recovers
institutional variation and that the pilot already contains useful comparisons
between high-capacity substantive cases and lower-capacity nominal cases.

The paper contributes to the study of local debt reform by disaggregating
official LGFV exit into substantive exit, nominal exit, functional transfer,
and liquidation. This distinction matters because aggregate reductions in
platform counts or reported debt can combine very different institutional
outcomes. The paper also develops a theoretical argument about formal fiscal
substitution. Local compliance with central fiscal formalization depends not
only on contemporary economic conditions or debt pressure, but also on
historically rooted administrative and fiscal capacity. The measurement strategy
adds an auditable LLM-assisted, human-validated coding procedure for studying
organizational change in settings where the key outcome is embedded in
heterogeneous administrative, financial, and corporate documents.
