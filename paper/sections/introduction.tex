\section{Introduction}

China's local government financing vehicles (LGFVs) have long occupied a
central position in local fiscal governance. They allowed local governments to
finance infrastructure, land development, and quasi-public investment outside
the formal budget. As land revenues declined and debt risks increased, the
central government began to require LGFVs to withdraw from government financing
functions. Official reports now describe large reductions in the number of
platform companies and in their outstanding operational financial debt. Yet
these aggregate figures leave open a crucial question: what does ``exit''
actually mean?

This paper argues that LGFV exit should not be treated as a binary event. An
official exit may indicate a genuine disappearance of government financing
functions, but it may also reflect nominal relabeling, organizational
restructuring, or the transfer of financing functions to other local
state-owned entities. The same central policy can therefore produce very
different institutional outcomes across localities. Some cities accomplish
substantive exit, while others comply formally but preserve the underlying
quasi-fiscal function through new arrangements.

The central question of this paper is: why do some cities achieve substantive
LGFV exit, while others exhibit nominal exit, functional transfer, or
liquidation? I argue that historically rooted local state capacity shapes the
type of exit localities can achieve. Cities with stronger historical state
capacity tend to possess more institutionalized fiscal administration, stronger
budgetary execution capacity, better project preparation capacity, and more
developed debt management routines. These capabilities make it easier to replace
informal platform financing with formal instruments such as special-purpose
bonds, on-budget infrastructure spending, and market-oriented restructuring.
Cities with weaker state capacity, by contrast, face stronger incentives to
maintain quasi-fiscal functions through nominal compliance or functional
transfer.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it offers a new measurement strategy
for studying LGFV exit by distinguishing among substantive exit, nominal exit,
functional transfer, and liquidation. Second, it links contemporary fiscal
formalization to historically rooted variation in local state capacity. Third,
it shows how large language models can be used as auxiliary tools in a
human-validated coding process, improving coding efficiency without replacing
researcher judgment.
