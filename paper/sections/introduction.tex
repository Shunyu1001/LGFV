\section{Introduction}

China's local government financing vehicle (LGFV) exit reform presents a
measurement problem with theoretical stakes. Official reports describe large
reductions in the number of platform companies and the volume of their
operational financial debt. These figures suggest progress in local debt
management. They do not show what happened to the government financing function
that platform companies previously performed. A company can be removed from an
official list, renamed, merged into a state capital operation company, or
declared market-oriented while its financing, construction, project-management,
or debt-rollover role remains in place. The central question is therefore not
only whether LGFV exit occurred. It is what kind of exit occurred.

This question matters because LGFVs have long occupied a central position in
local fiscal governance. They allowed local governments to finance
infrastructure, land development, industrial parks, urban construction, and
other quasi-public projects outside the ordinary budget
\citep{oi1992,oi1995,wong2013,tsui2011}. They also helped local governments
manage a persistent fiscal tension: local governments carry extensive
expenditure responsibilities, but they have faced tight formal constraints on
ordinary borrowing \citep{montinola1995,jin2005}. When land transfer revenues
were high and urban expansion generated large expected returns, platform finance
linked land development, infrastructure investment, and local debt. When land
revenues declined and repayment risks increased, the same arrangement became a
central object of fiscal and financial regulation
\citep{tsui2011,bai2016longshadow}.

This paper studies the institutional types of LGFV exit. By \emph{exit type}, I
mean the relationship between an official exit event and the post-event
location of the government financing function. I distinguish four types. First,
\emph{substantive exit} occurs when a platform company genuinely ceases to
perform government financing or quasi-fiscal financing functions. Second,
\emph{nominal exit} occurs when official exit or no-government-financing
language appears, but the same platform continues to perform similar functions.
Third, \emph{functional transfer} occurs when the original platform exits while
its financing, construction, or debt-management function moves to another local
state-owned or quasi-state entity. Fourth, liquidation occurs when the company
is dissolved, deregistered, bankrupted, or otherwise terminates its operations.
This classification shifts the analysis from official exit as a binary event
to the institutional content of exit.

The main argument is that historically rooted local state capacity shapes which
type of exit a locality can achieve. LGFV exit is not simply a regulatory
instruction to remove a label. It requires local governments to replace the
functions previously performed by platforms through formal fiscal instruments,
budgetary channels, regulated state assets, or genuinely commercial operations.
This process requires three capacities. First, fiscal absorption capacity lets a
locality classify platform obligations and move eligible public investment into
special-purpose bonds, budgetary spending, or other regulated fiscal channels.
Second, bureaucratic coordination capacity lets finance bureaus, development and
reform agencies, state-owned asset supervisors, creditors, and platform firms
agree on debt succession, asset transfers, and project reassignment. Third,
project and state-asset governance capacity lets local officials prepare
eligible projects, value and reorganize assets, and monitor whether post-exit
firms recreate hidden debt through entrusted construction, repurchase
agreements, subsidiaries, or fiscal subsidies. Compliance documents may reveal
these capacities, but they are not the mechanism itself.

Localities with stronger historically rooted administrative and fiscal capacity
should be more able to make this substitution. In these localities, official
exit is more likely to become substantive exit, or at least a regulated
reorganization of former platform functions. Localities with weaker capacity
should be more likely to preserve the old function through nominal compliance
or functional transfer. The argument therefore links a contemporary
debt-management reform to a longer problem in Chinese state building: whether
local fiscal activity can be moved from quasi-formal organizational devices
into visible, budgetary, and supervisable institutions.

Empirically, the paper develops a case-level coding strategy. The unit of
analysis is a city-platform case: a major LGFV or platform-like local
state-owned enterprise located within a prefecture-level city. For each case, I
code the formal exit event and the post-event location of the relevant function.
The evidence comes from government announcements, finance-bureau and
state-owned asset supervision documents, company disclosures, bond
prospectuses, credit rating reports, legal opinions, annual reports, business
registration records, and credible financial news. Large language models
generate preliminary labels under a fixed codebook, but final labels are
assigned only after human review of the original documents.

The current pilot contains twenty evidence packets: twelve human-validated
labels, four strong candidates, one boundary case, and three source-only
candidates. The validated labels already recover three distinct institutional
outcomes. Guangzhou and Ningbo are coded as substantive exit. Hangzhou,
Wenzhou, and Foshan are coded as functional transfer. Xi'an, Chengdu, Zhuzhou,
Xuzhou, Hengyang, Jinan, and Yancheng are coded as nominal exit. A small
descriptive comparison links these labels to historical-capacity bins: the
validated low-capacity cases are all nominal exits, while the high-capacity
cases include substantive exit, functional transfer, and nominal exit. This is
not yet a population estimate, but it shows that the coding scheme recovers
institutional variation and that the pilot already contains useful comparisons
between high-capacity substantive cases and lower-capacity nominal cases.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it offers a measurement
contribution to the study of local debt reform by disaggregating official LGFV
exit into substantive exit, nominal exit, functional transfer, and liquidation.
This distinction matters because aggregate reductions in platform counts or
reported debt can combine very different institutional outcomes. Second, it
develops a theoretical argument about formal fiscal substitution. Local
compliance with central fiscal formalization depends not only on contemporary
economic conditions or debt pressure, but also on historically rooted
administrative and fiscal capacity. Third, it introduces an auditable
LLM-assisted, human-validated coding procedure for studying organizational
change in settings where the key outcome is embedded in heterogeneous
administrative, financial, and corporate documents.
