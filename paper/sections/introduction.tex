\section{Introduction}

China's local government financing vehicles (LGFVs) have been central to the
political economy of local development. For more than a decade, they allowed
local governments to finance infrastructure, land development, industrial parks,
urban construction, and other quasi-public projects outside the ordinary budget
\citep{oi1992,oi1995,wong2013,tsui2011}.
This arrangement helped local governments reconcile two features of China's
fiscal system: extensive expenditure responsibilities at the local level and
tight formal constraints on local borrowing \citep{montinola1995,jin2005}.
When land transfer revenues were high and urban expansion generated large
expected returns, the platform model appeared to solve this problem by linking
land development, infrastructure investment, and local debt financing. When land
revenues declined and repayment risks increased, the same model became a
central object of fiscal and financial regulation \citep{tsui2011,bai2016longshadow}.

The central government has responded by requiring LGFVs to withdraw from
government financing functions. Recent official reports describe substantial
reductions in both the number of platform companies and the volume of their
operational financial debt. These aggregate figures suggest that the reform has
advanced rapidly, but they do not by themselves establish what has changed in
local fiscal governance. A platform company may be removed from an official
list, renamed, merged into a state capital operation company, or declared to
have completed market-oriented transformation, while the financing and project
functions it previously performed may either disappear, continue inside the same
firm, or move to another local state-owned entity. The empirical problem is
therefore not only whether a locality has exited. It is what kind of exit has
occurred.

This paper studies the institutional types of LGFV exit. By \emph{exit type}, I
mean the substantive relationship between an official exit event and the
post-exit location of the government financing function. I distinguish four
types. First, \emph{substantive exit} occurs when a platform company genuinely
ceases to perform government financing or quasi-fiscal functions. Second,
\emph{nominal exit} occurs when the company is officially removed from a list or
declared transformed but continues to perform similar functions. Third,
\emph{functional transfer} occurs when the original platform exits but its
financing, construction, or debt management function moves to another local
state-owned or quasi-state entity. Fourth, liquidation occurs when the company
is dissolved, deregistered, bankrupted, or otherwise ceases operations. This
classification shifts the analysis from a binary indicator of exit to the
institutional content of exit.

The main question is why the same central reform produces different exit types
across localities. I argue that historically rooted local state capacity shapes
the kind of exit that a locality can achieve. Cities with stronger historical
state capacity are more likely to have developed routinized fiscal
administration, stronger budgetary execution, better project preparation, and
more capable debt management \citep{besley2009,dincecco2017,johnson2017}.
These capacities matter because LGFV exit does not simply remove a firm from
the local public sector. It requires local governments to replace the functions
previously performed by that firm through formal fiscal instruments, regulated
state assets, or genuine market-oriented operations. Where local governments can
make this substitution, official exit is more likely to become substantive
exit. Where they cannot, formal compliance is more likely to coexist with
nominal continuation or functional transfer.

The argument links a contemporary fiscal reform to a longer problem in Chinese
state building. Central authorities have repeatedly attempted to standardize,
formalize, and supervise local fiscal behavior, while local officials have
needed flexible resources to provide public goods, maintain growth, and respond
to administrative mandates. LGFVs are a contemporary form of this older tension.
They converted local development obligations into organizational and financial
arrangements that stood near the boundary of the formal budget. The exit reform
is therefore not only a debt management policy. It is a recent instance of a
broader state project: the attempt to move local fiscal activity from informal
or quasi-formal instruments into more visible and supervisable institutions.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it offers a measurement strategy for
studying LGFV exit by disaggregating official exit into substantive exit,
nominal exit, functional transfer, and liquidation. This distinction is
important because the common binary measure conflates cases in which the
government financing function disappears with cases in which it is relabeled or
moved. Second, the paper develops an argument about formal fiscal substitution.
The ability to comply with central formalization depends not only on current
fiscal pressure or economic development, but also on administrative and fiscal
capacities that may have historical roots. Third, the paper develops a
transparent coding procedure for classifying exit types from government
announcements, company disclosures, bond prospectuses, credit rating reports,
and business registration records. Large language models are used as auxiliary
coding tools, but final classifications are assigned through human review of
the original documents.

The rest of the paper proceeds as follows. The next section positions the paper
in relation to work on Chinese fiscal decentralization, local debt, and state
capacity. The following section describes the background of LGFV expansion and
exit reform. The theory section then develops the distinction between official
exit and institutional exit and explains why historical state capacity should
affect the probability of substantive exit. The subsequent sections describe
the research design, the coding procedure, and the empirical strategy.
