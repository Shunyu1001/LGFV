\section{Theory}

The paper distinguishes between official exit and institutional exit. Official
exit refers to a formal designation that a platform company has withdrawn from
government financing functions or has completed market-oriented transformation.
Institutional exit refers to the substantive disappearance or transformation of
the quasi-fiscal function previously performed by the platform.

\subsection{Exit Types}

I classify LGFV exit into four types. Substantive exit occurs when a platform
company genuinely ceases performing government financing functions and either
becomes a market-oriented firm or no longer acts as a fiscal arm of the local
state. Nominal exit occurs when the company is removed from official lists or
declared transformed but continues to perform similar government financing,
infrastructure construction, or public project functions. Functional transfer
occurs when the original platform exits but its fiscal or financing function is
shifted to another local state-owned enterprise, subsidiary, new entity, or
quasi-state organization. Liquidation occurs when the company is dissolved,
bankrupted, deregistered, or otherwise ceases operations.

\subsection{Historical State Capacity}

Historical state capacity refers to durable local administrative and fiscal
capacity formed through long-run processes of state building. Historically
stronger localities may have developed denser administrative institutions,
better fiscal extraction capacity, more routinized bureaucratic governance, and
stronger links between state institutions and local society. These historical
features may persist through contemporary fiscal administration, budgetary
management, project preparation, debt governance, and compliance capacity.

The core mechanism is formal fiscal substitution. Cities with stronger state
capacity are better able to move functions previously performed by LGFVs into
formal fiscal channels. They can prepare eligible projects for special-purpose
bond financing, manage on-budget infrastructure expenditure, restructure local
state assets, and comply with debt supervision requirements. These localities
are therefore more likely to achieve substantive exit.

By contrast, cities with weaker state capacity are more dependent on flexible
quasi-fiscal tools. When formal instruments are insufficient, they may preserve
LGFV-like functions through nominal relabeling or functional transfer. The
result is formal compliance without substantive institutional change.

\subsection{Hypotheses}

\textbf{H1:} Cities with stronger historical state capacity are more likely to
achieve substantive LGFV exit.

\textbf{H2:} Cities with weaker historical state capacity are more likely to
display nominal exit or functional transfer.

\textbf{H3:} The relationship between historical state capacity and exit type is
mediated by contemporary fiscal substitution capacity, including special-purpose
bond absorption, on-budget infrastructure spending, project preparation, and
debt management capacity.
