\section{Theory}

The previous section introduced the distinction between official exit and exit
type. This section develops the theoretical argument. The central claim is that
LGFV exit requires more than formal compliance with a central directive. It
requires local governments to replace, reorganize, or discontinue functions that
platform companies previously performed. Because these tasks differ in their
administrative and fiscal requirements, local state capacity should affect the
type of exit that localities can achieve.

\subsection{Official and institutional exit}

I distinguish between \emph{official exit} and \emph{institutional exit}.
Official exit refers to a formal administrative or documentary status: a
platform company is removed from an official list, declared to have withdrawn
from government financing functions, or described as having completed
market-oriented transformation. Institutional exit refers to the substantive
change in the quasi-fiscal function previously performed by the platform. The
two concepts are related, but they are not identical. Official exit is observed
in documents and regulatory classifications. Institutional exit must be inferred
from the post-exit allocation of financing, construction, project management,
and debt rollover functions.

This distinction matters because LGFVs are not ordinary firms in local fiscal
politics. They are organizational devices through which local governments have
financed projects, managed development obligations, and connected land,
infrastructure, credit, and state assets. A reform that changes the legal or
administrative status of the platform may or may not change this fiscal role.
If the platform no longer borrows for the government and former projects are
absorbed by formal fiscal instruments, official exit corresponds to
institutional exit. If the platform remains responsible for public projects or
if the same function is transferred to another local state-owned entity,
official exit changes the organizational label without eliminating the
underlying function.

\subsection{Exit types}

The paper classifies LGFV exit into four types. This classification is designed
to capture the post-exit location of the government financing function rather
than the language used in official announcements.

First, substantive exit occurs when the platform genuinely ceases to perform
government financing or quasi-fiscal functions. In this case, the company may
continue to exist as a market-oriented firm, but it no longer acts as an
off-budget fiscal arm of the local government. Evidence for substantive exit
includes a decline in public project financing, a reduction in fiscal support
arrangements, a shift toward commercial revenue, and the absorption of former
platform functions by formal budgetary instruments.

Second, nominal exit occurs when the company is officially removed from a list
or declared transformed but continues to perform similar functions. The
observable form changes, but the substantive relationship between the firm and
the local government remains similar. This category is likely when post-exit
documents continue to describe infrastructure construction, land development,
public welfare projects, fiscal subsidies, asset injections, or repayment
support from the local state.

Third, functional transfer occurs when the original platform exits but its
financing or project function moves to another local state-owned enterprise,
subsidiary, merged entity, newly established group company, or quasi-state
organization. Unlike nominal exit, the continuing function is not necessarily
located in the same firm. The relevant evidence lies in reorganizations, asset
transfers, new debt issuers, new project companies, or changes in the identity
of the local entity that carries out infrastructure and financing tasks.

Fourth, liquidation occurs when the company is dissolved, deregistered,
bankrupted, or otherwise ceases operations. Liquidation is conceptually
distinct from substantive exit because it describes the disappearance of the
organizational entity, not necessarily the formal absorption of its function by
the budget. In some cases, liquidation may follow from a genuine end of the
platform's fiscal role. In other cases, it may coexist with functional transfer
if another local entity assumes the same responsibilities.

\subsection{Formal fiscal substitution}

The key mechanism is formal fiscal substitution. By formal fiscal substitution,
I mean the process through which a local government replaces platform-based
quasi-fiscal financing with formal fiscal instruments, regulated state asset
management, or genuinely commercial operations. This process has three
components. First, local governments must identify which projects can be funded
through special-purpose bonds, budgetary expenditure, or other regulated
instruments. Second, they must reorganize the assets, liabilities, and project
obligations attached to existing platform companies. Third, they must maintain
investment and public service provision without recreating the same implicit
debt arrangements through another entity.

These tasks are administratively demanding. Special-purpose bonds require
eligible projects, expected revenue streams, documentation, and compliance with
central rules. Budgetary absorption requires fiscal planning and expenditure
discipline. Debt restructuring requires coordination among finance bureaus,
state-owned asset supervisors, creditors, rating agencies, and platform
companies. Market-oriented transformation requires the platform to develop
commercial revenue sources rather than rely primarily on government support.
For this reason, substantive exit is not simply a choice to comply. It is an
outcome that depends on whether local governments have the capacity to replace
an informal fiscal instrument with formal institutions.

Nominal exit and functional transfer arise when this substitution is incomplete.
If local officials face pressure to meet central targets but cannot move
projects and liabilities into formal channels, they may preserve the function
inside the same company while changing its official status. If regulatory
pressure is directed at a specific platform company or list of platforms, they
may move the function to another local state-owned entity. In both cases, the
locality can display formal compliance while continuing to rely on quasi-fiscal
arrangements for development and debt management.

\subsection{Historical state capacity}

Historical state capacity refers to durable local administrative and fiscal
capacity formed through long-run processes of state building. In the Chinese
context, candidate indicators include administrative density, proximity to
historical administrative centers, imperial examination density, historical tax
capacity, transportation access, and other measures of the historical reach of
the state into local society. These measures do not imply that Qing-era
institutions mechanically determine contemporary fiscal behavior. The claim is
more modest: historically stronger localities may have developed administrative
routines, fiscal practices, elite-state linkages, and organizational resources
that persist, in altered form, into contemporary governance.

The connection between historical capacity and LGFV exit operates through
contemporary administrative and fiscal capacity. Historically stronger
localities are more likely to have capable finance bureaus, more routinized
budget management, stronger project preparation capacity, and more experienced
state asset management agencies. These localities can more readily prepare
eligible projects for special-purpose bonds, reorganize platform assets and
liabilities, and monitor the boundary between commercial operations and
government financing. As a result, they are more likely to convert official
exit into substantive exit.

Historically weaker localities face a different problem. They may have relied
more heavily on flexible organizational devices because formal fiscal resources
and administrative routines are insufficient for the tasks assigned to local
governments. When the central government restricts LGFV financing, these
localities still need to maintain infrastructure investment, service existing
debts, and carry out development projects. If formal instruments do not provide
enough capacity to replace the platform, local officials have stronger
incentives to preserve the function through nominal exit or functional
transfer.

\subsection{Hypotheses}

The argument generates three hypotheses. First, cities with stronger historical
state capacity should be more likely to achieve substantive LGFV exit. This
does not mean that they face less fiscal pressure, but rather that they have
greater administrative and fiscal capacity to move former platform functions
into formal channels.

Second, cities with weaker historical state capacity should be more likely to
display nominal exit or functional transfer. In these places, official exit can
serve as a response to central pressure while the underlying financing function
continues inside the same firm or another local state-owned entity.

Third, the relationship between historical state capacity and exit type should
be mediated by contemporary fiscal substitution capacity. Cities with stronger
historical capacity should display greater use of formal instruments such as
special-purpose bonds, stronger budgetary absorption of infrastructure
spending, better project preparation, and less evidence of new quasi-fiscal
debt accumulation outside the original platform sector.
