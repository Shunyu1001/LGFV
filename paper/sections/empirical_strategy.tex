\section{Empirical Strategy}

\subsection{Main specifications}

The baseline empirical analysis estimates whether historical state capacity
predicts the probability of substantive exit. A simple specification is:

\begin{equation}
SubstantiveExit_i = \alpha + \beta HistoricalCapacity_i + X_i'\gamma + \epsilon_i,
\end{equation}

where $SubstantiveExit_i$ indicates whether city-platform case $i$ achieved
substantive exit, $HistoricalCapacity_i$ measures historically rooted state
capacity in the relevant locality, and $X_i$ includes contemporary fiscal,
economic, debt, and political controls.

The first version of $HistoricalCapacity_i$ will be constructed from CHGIS
\citep{chgis2024}. The main measure is Qing administrative density: the number
of late-imperial county or prefectural administrative units intersecting the
contemporary prefecture-level city, scaled by land area. This measure captures
the depth of historical administrative presence rather than contemporary fiscal
strength. The main alternative measure will use CBDB to construct Ming-Qing
elite density, especially the density of high-degree civil service examination
holders by place of affiliation \citep{cbdb2024,bai2016elite}. The first
operational version counts jinshi and juren records by historical place,
assigns those places to contemporary prefecture-level cities using their
coordinates and modern administrative boundaries, and scales the resulting
counts by prefecture land area. This second measure captures local
administrative human capital and elite formation. A third measure, based on
Ming courier routes and stations, can be used as a robustness check for
historical communication and transport infrastructure.

A second specification uses exit type as a multinomial outcome:

\begin{equation}
Pr(ExitType_i = k) = f(HistoricalCapacity_i, X_i),
\end{equation}

where $k$ indexes substantive exit, nominal exit, functional transfer, and
liquidation. This specification evaluates whether historical state capacity is
associated with different institutional responses to the same central reform.

\subsection{Pilot evidence}

The first empirical step is an expanded pilot sample. The purpose of the pilot
is not to estimate the main effect. The purpose is to show that the exit-type
categories can be assigned from original documents, that the categories
distinguish different institutional outcomes, and that the cases span
meaningful variation in historical state capacity. For this reason, I separate
human-validated labels from candidate cases throughout the analysis.

Table \ref{tab:pilot-validation-tiers} summarizes the validation status of the
current evidence packets. The human-validated label file now contains
fifty-nine cases, and the broader screening file contains 247 disclosure rows,
including four boundary-reviewed packets. The descriptive tables in this section retain
the historical-capacity matched subset until the remaining expansion cases are
merged into the historical-capacity crosswalk. This keeps the pilot evidence
auditable while making clear that source collection has moved beyond the
original twelve-case demonstration.

Case comparisons clarify the mechanism. Guangzhou and Ningbo illustrate
high-capacity substantive exit. In Guangzhou,
the source packet shows a shift away from new government project investment and
financing and toward fiscally funded project management and debt replacement
through budgetary channels. In Ningbo, the issuer's disclosures combine
no-government-financing language with negative evidence on core platform
functions: no major PPP, BT, government purchase service, land preparation,
advance financing, or fiscal repayment arrangement appears in the reviewed bond
materials. Xuzhou, Yancheng, and Hengyang point in the opposite direction.
Their documents contain formal exit or no-government-financing language, but
the same issuers continued to show shantytown redevelopment, entrusted
construction, land preparation, government settlement, public project
repayment, or fiscal subsidy arrangements. These contrasts do not claim that
historical capacity mechanically determines exit. They show the observable
implication of the mechanism: substantive exit requires more than compliant
language, while nominal exit can satisfy formal reporting demands without
moving the underlying fiscal function.

Shijiazhuang Transportation Investment adds a middle-capacity transport
contrast. The issuer's 2026 prospectus states that the company does not
undertake government financing functions and that new debt after January 1,
2015 is not local-government debt. The same packet shows that the issuer and
its subsidiaries remain central to municipal expressway planning,
construction, operation, PPP and BOT arrangements, toll-right pledges,
government subsidies, and state-asset injections. The case therefore
reinforces the measurement claim: formal no-hidden-debt language can be legally
meaningful while the public infrastructure finance function remains
institutionally present.

Nanjing Transportation Construction Investment adds a high-capacity
counterpart to this transport-platform pattern. Its prospectus and legal
opinion separate the issuer from local-government debt and deny a government
financing function, but the same packet describes the group as Nanjing's main
urban transport construction and road-bridge operation body. It records
municipal capital injections, SASAC transfers, road, bridge, expressway,
railway-supporting and station-supporting projects, government-related
receivables, toll-right pledges, fiscal subsidies, and transport-infrastructure
debt pressure. This case is important because it prevents a mechanical reading
of the theory. High historical capacity does not guarantee substantive exit.
It can also support a legally cleaner but still functionally persistent
transport platform.

Haian Tenghai Investment adds a county-level development-zone case. The formal
record contains the usual no-government-financing and no-local-government-debt
language, reinforced by a legal opinion. Yet the same documents describe
Tenghai as a major investment and construction body for the Haian Economic and
Technological Development Zone, with housing construction, infrastructure
construction, municipal engineering, government receivables, fiscal cash-flow
dependence, special project funds, and state-asset support. This case extends
the same measurement logic below the prefecture level: formal compliance can
coexist with continued public-development functions in county and development
zone platforms.

Gaochun International Cittaslow Cultural Tourism Investment adds a
high-capacity district cultural-tourism platform. The formal documents contain
the same post-2015 compliance language and a legal opinion denying new
government debt or hidden debt. The same source packet, however, describes a
company responsible for investment, financing, construction, and management of
tourism infrastructure around Gucheng Lake, Gaochun Old Street, Yaxi
International Cittaslow, and Youzi Mountain. Its infrastructure construction
is implemented through entrusted-construction contracts with the tourism-zone
management committee, while government subsidies, government-related
receivables, negative operating cash flow, and payment-timing risk remain
visible. The case shows again that high historical capacity can produce
legally cleaner disclosure without removing the public-development role from
the issuer group.

Jiaxing Jiaxiu Development Investment adds an even sharper high-capacity
nominal-exit case. Jiaxing sits at the top of the elite-density distribution
in the matched historical-capacity data. The source packet nevertheless places
no-government-financing language beside Xiuzhou infrastructure, resettlement
housing, land preparation, entrusted construction, government-related
receivables, fiscal subsidies, and fiscal settlement. The case reinforces the
paper's interpretation of historical capacity as a constraint on the feasible
set of local responses, rather than a deterministic cause of substantive exit.

The pilot therefore makes a limited but useful empirical contribution. It does
not yet support a regression estimate, and it should not be read as a

coding rule separates substantive exit, nominal exit, and functional transfer
in real documents, and that the next stage of the project can target cases that
vary sharply in historical administrative human capital.

\subsection{Inference with surrogate labels}

The scaled version of the project separates three objects that are often
collapsed in text-as-data applications. The first is the human-validated label,
which is the gold-standard outcome. The second is the Codex or LLM-generated
label, which is an imperfect surrogate for that outcome. The third is the
source-packet status, which records whether a candidate disclosure contains
enough source text to support any label at all. This distinction is important
because measurement error is not likely to be random. A model may overread
formal compliance language as substantive exit, miss continuing public
functions buried in bond documents, or produce no useful label when the
candidate file contains only a disclosure-page title.

The current screening file makes this separation explicit. It contains
fifty-nine human-validated gold-standard cases, four boundary-reviewed packets,
and 184 pending candidate cases. Applying the frozen codebook through a
conservative Codex screening pass produces 36 surrogate labels and 148
unresolved candidates. The surrogate labels are medium-confidence nominal-exit
cases because the source packets pair no-government-financing language with
continued evidence of public development, infrastructure construction,
entrusted projects, land development, fiscal support, or state-asset functions.
They correspond to 23 unique issuers because repeated
bond disclosures often describe the same platform. The analysis therefore
treats them as disclosure-level surrogate labels until they are deduplicated
or aggregated to the city-platform level. At the issuer level, all 23 of these
issuers exactly match platforms already present in the gold-standard file under
different case IDs. This overlap is useful for checking whether the surrogate
procedure recovers already validated nominal-exit cases, but it is not counted
as new validation expansion. The current medium-confidence surrogate pass
therefore leaves no non-overlap issuer in the human-review queue. The
unresolved cases remain
unresolved for two reasons: some have source packets but no direct formal exit
or compliance event, while others still need source-packet collection. These
rows are retained for source collection but are not treated as outcomes.

For inference, I follow the design-based supervised learning logic of
\citet{egami2023dsl}. Let $Y_i$ denote the unobserved gold-standard exit label,
$S_i$ the LLM surrogate label, and $V_i$ an indicator for whether case $i$ is
human validated. The validation sample is drawn with known sampling
probabilities and oversamples low-capacity and high-debt localities, where
measurement risk is greatest. A prediction model uses $S_i$, source coverage,
document type, candidate priority, historical capacity, debt pressure, land
finance dependence, and platform characteristics to estimate the gold-standard
outcome. The downstream estimand, such as the association between historical
capacity and substantive exit, is then estimated with a validation-adjusted or
doubly robust correction rather than with raw surrogate labels.

This procedure changes the role of the model. The LLM is not the final coder,
and its accuracy is not the main quantity of interest. The model helps expand
the candidate pool and provides auxiliary information for prediction. Human
labels identify the outcome scale and estimate the error structure. Cases
without usable source packets enter the workflow as missing-label candidates
rather than as negative outcomes. For the binary specification, the corrected
estimand is the relationship between historical capacity and
$SubstantiveExit_i$. For the multinomial specification, I estimate
type-specific indicators for substantive exit, nominal exit, functional
transfer, and liquidation. In both cases, uncertainty must reflect the
validation design and the fact that the large candidate pool is measured
through imperfect surrogates.

\subsection{Mechanism analyses}

Mechanism analyses examine whether stronger historical state capacity is
associated with greater formal fiscal substitution after exit. Relevant
outcomes correspond to the three capacities in the theory section. Fiscal
absorption can be measured through special-purpose bond use, on-budget
infrastructure spending, and the movement of former platform projects into
budgetary channels. Bureaucratic coordination can be measured through formal
asset transfers, debt-succession arrangements, municipal platform-group
reorganizations, and cross-agency implementation documents. Project and
state-asset governance can be measured through eligible project preparation,
fiscal-settlement rules, reductions in entrusted-construction receivables,
changes in non-platform local SOE debt, and evidence that post-exit firms no
longer recreate implicit government financing. These outcomes are not the main
dependent variables of the paper; instead, they help explain why some
localities are able to achieve substantive exit while others rely on responses
such as nominal exit or functional transfer.
