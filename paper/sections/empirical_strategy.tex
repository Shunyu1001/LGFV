\section{Empirical Strategy}

The baseline empirical analysis estimates whether historical state capacity
predicts the probability of substantive exit. A simple specification is:

\begin{equation}
SubstantiveExit_i = \alpha + \beta HistoricalCapacity_i + X_i'\gamma + \epsilon_i,
\end{equation}

where $SubstantiveExit_i$ indicates whether city or platform $i$ achieved
substantive exit, $HistoricalCapacity_i$ measures historically rooted state
capacity, and $X_i$ includes contemporary fiscal, economic, debt, and political
controls.

The first version of $HistoricalCapacity_i$ will be constructed from CHGIS. The
main measure is Qing administrative density: the number of late-imperial county
or prefectural administrative units intersecting the contemporary
prefecture-level city, scaled by land area. This measure captures the depth of
historical administrative presence rather than contemporary fiscal strength.
The main alternative measure will use CBDB to construct Ming-Qing elite density,
especially the density of high-degree civil service examination holders by
place of affiliation. The first operational version counts jinshi and juren
records by historical place, assigns those places to contemporary
prefecture-level cities using their coordinates and modern administrative
boundaries, and scales the resulting counts by prefecture land area. This
second measure captures local administrative human capital and elite formation.
A third measure, based on Ming courier routes and stations, can be used as a
robustness check for historical communication and transport infrastructure.

As a pilot validation exercise, I match the current human-validated cases and
three candidate cases to the CBDB-GADM elite-density measure. This exercise is
not a test of the main hypothesis, because the pilot cases were chosen to
develop and stress-test the coding procedure rather than to form a
representative sample. It is nevertheless useful as a check on whether the
historical measure captures substantively meaningful variation across the kinds
of cities that appear in the source-collection process. Table
\ref{tab:pilot-historical-capacity} and Figure
\ref{fig:pilot-historical-capacity} show the resulting values. Guangzhou and
Hangzhou have high historical elite density, Chengdu and Xi'an occupy an
intermediate position, and the low-capacity cases---Luzhou, Zhuzhou, Zunyi,
and Liupanshui---have much lower values. The key point is not that the pilot
already estimates an effect. Rather, the pilot shows that the coding scheme is
being applied to cases that vary sharply in historical administrative human
capital, including a validated low-capacity nominal-exit case in Zhuzhou and a
near-miss low-capacity functional-persistence case in Luzhou.

A second specification uses exit type as a multinomial outcome:

\begin{equation}
Pr(ExitType_i = k) = f(HistoricalCapacity_i, X_i),
\end{equation}

where $k$ indexes substantive exit, nominal exit, functional transfer, and
liquidation. This model directly evaluates whether historical state capacity is
associated with different institutional responses to the same central reform.

Mechanism tests examine whether stronger historical state capacity is associated
with greater formal fiscal substitution after exit. Relevant outcomes include
special-purpose bond use, on-budget infrastructure spending, project pipeline
readiness, debt restructuring capacity, and changes in non-platform local SOE
debt. These outcomes are not the main dependent variables of the paper; instead,
they help explain why some localities are able to achieve substantive exit while
others rely on adaptive workarounds.
