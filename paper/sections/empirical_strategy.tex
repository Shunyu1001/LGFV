\section{Empirical Strategy}

\subsection{Main specifications}

The baseline empirical analysis estimates whether historical state capacity
predicts the probability of substantive exit. A simple specification is:

\begin{equation}
SubstantiveExit_i = \alpha + \beta HistoricalCapacity_i + X_i'\gamma + \epsilon_i,
\end{equation}

where $SubstantiveExit_i$ indicates whether city-platform case $i$ achieved
substantive exit, $HistoricalCapacity_i$ measures historically rooted state
capacity in the relevant locality, and $X_i$ includes contemporary fiscal,
economic, debt, and political controls.

The first version of $HistoricalCapacity_i$ will be constructed from CHGIS
\citep{chgis2024}. The main measure is Qing administrative density: the number
of late-imperial county or prefectural administrative units intersecting the
contemporary prefecture-level city, scaled by land area. This measure captures
the depth of historical administrative presence rather than contemporary fiscal
strength. The main alternative measure will use CBDB to construct Ming-Qing
elite density, especially the density of high-degree civil service examination
holders by place of affiliation \citep{cbdb2024,bai2016elite}. The first
operational version counts jinshi and juren records by historical place,
assigns those places to contemporary prefecture-level cities using their
coordinates and modern administrative boundaries, and scales the resulting
counts by prefecture land area. This second measure captures local
administrative human capital and elite formation. A third measure, based on
Ming courier routes and stations, can be used as a robustness check for
historical communication and transport infrastructure.

A second specification uses exit type as a multinomial outcome:

\begin{equation}
Pr(ExitType_i = k) = f(HistoricalCapacity_i, X_i),
\end{equation}

where $k$ indexes substantive exit, nominal exit, functional transfer, and
liquidation. This specification evaluates whether historical state capacity is
associated with different institutional responses to the same central reform.

\subsection{Pilot evidence}

The first empirical step is an expanded pilot sample. The purpose of the pilot
is not to estimate the main effect. The purpose is to show that the exit-type
categories can be assigned from original documents, that the categories
distinguish different institutional outcomes, and that the cases span
meaningful variation in historical state capacity. For this reason, I separate
human-validated labels from candidate cases throughout the analysis.

Table \ref{tab:pilot-validation-tiers} summarizes the validation status of the
current evidence packets. The pilot now contains twenty cases. Twelve are
human-validated labels. Four are strong candidates with enough source material
to support a provisional interpretation, but not enough to enter the final
label file. One is a boundary case that separates historical capacity from
contemporary administrative and fiscal strength. Three are source-only
candidate cases that document useful evidence trails but do not yet contain the
formal event needed for final labeling.

Case comparisons clarify the mechanism. Guangzhou and Ningbo illustrate
high-capacity substantive exit. In Guangzhou,
the source packet shows a shift away from new government project investment and
financing and toward fiscally funded project management and debt replacement
through budgetary channels. In Ningbo, the issuer's disclosures combine
no-government-financing language with negative evidence on core platform
functions: no major PPP, BT, government purchase service, land preparation,
advance financing, or fiscal repayment arrangement appears in the reviewed bond
materials. Xuzhou, Yancheng, and Hengyang point in the opposite direction.
Their documents contain formal exit or no-government-financing language, but
the same issuers continued to show shantytown redevelopment, entrusted
construction, land preparation, government settlement, public project
repayment, or fiscal subsidy arrangements. These contrasts do not claim that
historical capacity mechanically determines exit. They show the observable
implication of the mechanism: substantive exit requires more than compliant
language, while nominal exit can satisfy formal reporting demands without
moving the underlying fiscal function.

The pilot therefore makes a limited but useful empirical contribution. It does
not yet support a regression estimate, and it should not be read as a
representative distribution of LGFV exit types. It does show that the proposed
coding rule separates substantive exit, nominal exit, and functional transfer
in real documents, and that the next stage of the project can target cases that
vary sharply in historical administrative human capital.

\subsection{Inference with surrogate labels}

The large-sample version of the project will use LLM-assisted labels before all
candidate cases can be fully human-validated. Directly using these labels as if
they were true outcomes would create a measurement problem, because LLM errors
may be correlated with source quality, city capacity, document type, or the
visibility of formal compliance language. I therefore treat LLM labels as
imperfect surrogate labels and use human-validated labels as gold-standard
outcomes for inference.

The main inferential strategy will follow the design-based supervised learning
approach proposed by \citet{egami2023dsl}. I draw a validation sample of
city-platform cases for human labeling with known sampling probabilities, so
that the validation design becomes part of the estimator rather than an
after-the-fact quality check. The LLM labels, source metadata, and case
covariates are then used to predict gold-standard labels in the larger
candidate sample. The prediction model is combined with the validation sample
through a doubly robust correction, so that the downstream regression or
descriptive estimand is adjusted for surrogate-label error.

The quantities of interest are downstream relationships rather than the
prediction accuracy of the LLM labels. These include the association between
historical state capacity and substantive exit, as well as the distribution of
exit types across historical-capacity groups. For the binary specification, the
DSL estimator can be applied directly to
$SubstantiveExit_i$. For the multinomial specification, I can construct one
indicator for each exit type and estimate the corresponding type-specific
relationship, with uncertainty that reflects both sampling variation and label
uncertainty. The human-validated label file remains the authoritative source
for final case labels; the DSL procedure provides a way to use the larger
LLM-coded candidate set without treating surrogate labels as error-free.

\subsection{Mechanism analyses}

Mechanism analyses examine whether stronger historical state capacity is
associated with greater formal fiscal substitution after exit. Relevant
outcomes correspond to the three capacities in the theory section. Fiscal
absorption can be measured through special-purpose bond use, on-budget
infrastructure spending, and the movement of former platform projects into
budgetary channels. Bureaucratic coordination can be measured through formal
asset transfers, debt-succession arrangements, municipal platform-group
reorganizations, and cross-agency implementation documents. Project and
state-asset governance can be measured through eligible project preparation,
fiscal-settlement rules, reductions in entrusted-construction receivables,
changes in non-platform local SOE debt, and evidence that post-exit firms no
longer recreate implicit government financing. These outcomes are not the main
dependent variables of the paper; instead, they help explain why some
localities are able to achieve substantive exit while others rely on responses
such as nominal exit or functional transfer.
