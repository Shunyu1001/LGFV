\section{Empirical Evidence}

The previous sections defined LGFV exit as an institutional fate rather than
as an official status. This section turns that definition into empirical
evidence. The evidence supports three claims. First, official exit language
does not by itself identify functional fiscal change. Second, the validated
labels reveal systematic variation in where the public financing function goes
after formal exit. Third, historically rooted state capacity is associated
with movement away from narrow nominal compliance in simple specifications,
but this association attenuates after the current first-pass contemporary
controls are added. The main empirical contribution at this stage is therefore
measurement and validation. The historical-capacity models show how the
measure can be used, but they should not yet be read as a final causal
estimate.

I keep three empirical objects separate throughout the section. The first is
the gold-standard label file, which contains human-validated city-platform
cases. The second is the historically matched subset of those labels, which is
used to study the relationship between exit type and historical capacity. The
third is the issuer-level LLM surrogate file, which expands descriptive
coverage under a validation design but does not replace human labels.

\subsection{Validated measurement sample}

The current gold-standard file contains ninety-four human-validated
city-platform cases. Each label is assigned from original or near-original
source documents rather than from an aggregate exit count, a platform list, or
a single compliance sentence. The label file records the formal event, the
post-event location of the relevant function, the final exit type, the
plausible alternative label, the source coverage score, and the rationale for
the coding decision. The broader screening file contains 361 candidate
disclosure rows. These rows are useful for expanding the source base, but they
are not treated as final outcomes until they pass the coding rule and human
review.

Table \ref{tab:pilot-validation-tiers} summarizes the current measurement
base. Eighty-four of the ninety-four validated labels can be matched to the
CBDB-GADM historical-capacity crosswalk. Ten validated cases remain unmatched
because the present crosswalk does not yet provide a usable city or county
match. The empirical tables that compare exit type to historical capacity use
the matched subset. The full label file remains the gold-standard outcome file
for validation, source auditing, and future sample expansion.

The validated labels show why exit should be treated as a measurement problem.
The gold-standard file contains two substantive exits, eighty-two nominal
exits, and ten functional transfers. No liquidation case has entered the
gold-standard file. This distribution should not be interpreted as a
population share. The sample was built to validate the categories and to
stress-test difficult source packets, not to estimate the national frequency
of each outcome. Its main lesson is that formal exit language commonly
coexists with continuing public-project, fiscal-support, land-development,
entrusted-construction, repayment, or state-asset functions.

The source audit is central to this claim. Appendix Table
\ref{tab:pilot-source-audit} reports the primary source, formal event,
continued-function evidence, and final label for the historically matched
validated cases. A prospectus may state that new debt does not constitute
local government debt, while other sections of the same document describe
infrastructure investment, fiscal receivables, entrusted construction,
government-linked repayment, or continuing land-development obligations. The
label is therefore assigned from a joint reading of formal-event evidence and
post-event functional evidence. Official no-government-financing language is
evidence, but it is not the outcome.

\subsection{Exit type variation}

Table \ref{tab:pilot-capacity-bin-exit-type} reports the core descriptive
pattern in the historically matched validated sample. The table groups cases
into low, middle, and high historical-capacity bins using the CBDB-GADM
Ming-Qing elite-density measure. The low-capacity bin contains only nominal
exits. The middle bin contains nominal exits and functional transfers. The
high-capacity bin contains all three observed outcomes, including both
substantive-exit cases.

\input{tables/pilot_capacity_bin_exit_type}

This pattern supports a restrained version of the theoretical claim.
Historical state capacity does not mechanically produce substantive exit.
Many high-capacity cases still receive nominal-exit labels. What changes is
the feasible institutional menu. Higher-capacity localities are more likely to
show some movement away from pure formal compliance, either by absorbing former
platform functions into fiscal channels or by reorganizing those functions
through a local state-owned system. Lower-capacity cases in the current
validated sample are dominated by formal compliance paired with continuing
platform functions.

Figure \ref{fig:pilot-exit-type-by-capacity-bin} presents the same comparison
as shares. This figure replaces a ranking of historical elite density because
the outcome categories, rather than the capacity measure alone, are the object
of interest. Substantive exit is rare in the current validated sample, but
when it appears it appears only in the high-capacity bin. Functional transfer
appears in the middle and high bins. Nominal exit dominates all bins and fully
dominates the low-capacity bin.

The case evidence clarifies what the categories mean. Guangzhou City
Construction Investment and Ningbo Development Investment are coded as
substantive exit because the reviewed documents indicate movement away from
off-budget government-project financing. In Guangzhou, former government
project work is described as fiscally funded entrusted management rather than
platform financing. In Ningbo, the reviewed source packet does not show the
continuing public-project financing categories that would rule out
substantive exit. Xuzhou, Yancheng, Hengyang, Shijiazhuang, and Zhengzhou
point in the opposite direction. Their documents contain formal exit or
no-government-financing language, but the same issuer or platform system
continues to show infrastructure construction, land preparation, government
settlement, fiscal subsidy, public-capital support, repayment exposure, or
entrusted-construction functions.

The high-capacity nominal-exit cases are especially important because they
keep the argument from becoming deterministic. Nanjing Transportation
Construction Investment, Haian Tenghai Investment, Gaochun International
Cittaslow Cultural Tourism Investment, Jiaxing Jiaxiu Development Investment,
Suzhou Wuzhong, Shaoxing, Huzhou, Beijing Future Science City, and Zhengzhou
Airport Economy Zone all come from middle- or high-capacity settings, yet
their source packets support nominal-exit labels. These cases show that
historical capacity does not guarantee substantive exit. It changes the
institutional options available to local officials, but development-zone
functions, platform hierarchy, debt pressure, land dependence, and local
investment mandates can still preserve platform-like fiscal activity.

\subsection{Association with historical capacity}

The first statistical test uses the historically matched validated cases. The
dependent variable is institutional change, which equals one for substantive
exit or functional transfer and zero for nominal exit. This binary outcome is
not meant to collapse the theory. Substantive exit and functional transfer are
different institutional fates. The purpose of the specification is narrower:
it asks whether historical capacity predicts movement away from the narrowest
form of official compliance, given that the current gold-standard file
contains only two substantive-exit cases.

\input{tables/pilot_lpm_institutional_change}

Table \ref{tab:pilot-lpm-institutional-change} reports linear probability
models with robust standard errors. Across the simple validated-sample
specifications, historical capacity is positively associated with
institutional change. A one-standard-deviation increase in elite density is
associated with a 6.3 percentage point increase in the probability of
substantive exit or functional transfer. Membership in the high
historical-capacity bin is associated with a 17.1 percentage point increase
relative to other bins. Moving from low to middle to high capacity is
associated with a 13.5 percentage point increase at each step.

These estimates should be interpreted as descriptive associations. They are
useful for two reasons. First, they show that the theoretical pattern is not
only a narrative contrast between a few named cases. Second, they define the
empirical specification that the full sample will use once contemporary
fiscal, debt, land-finance, and platform-level controls are complete. They do
not, by themselves, establish that historical state capacity has a stable
adjusted effect across the population of LGFV exits.

\subsection{Contemporary controls}

The main alternative explanation is contemporary fiscal strength. Wealthier
cities may be able to move platform functions onto budget, replace implicit
debt with formal debt instruments, and maintain investment without relying on
LGFVs, regardless of historical state capacity. A second explanation is debt
pressure. Highly indebted cities may announce exit because central discipline
requires it, while preserving platform functions because fiscal stress makes
substantive exit difficult. A third explanation is land finance dependence:
localities that rely heavily on land development may keep platform functions
alive even after formal exit. Other concerns include province-level policy
differences, bond market disclosure quality, platform administrative rank, and
capital or sub-provincial status.

The empirical specification therefore uses the following form:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
InstitutionalChange_i ={}& \alpha + \beta HistoricalCapacity_i \\
&+ X_i'\gamma + \delta_p + \epsilon_i ,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The dependent variable equals one for substantive exit or functional transfer.
Historical capacity is measured with historical administrative or elite
density. The control vector includes contemporary fiscal capacity, debt
pressure, land finance dependence, bond disclosure quality, platform
administrative level, and capital or sub-provincial status. Province fixed
effects are included when the sample supports them. The multinomial version
uses substantive exit, nominal exit, functional transfer, and liquidation as
separate outcomes:

\begin{equation}
Pr(ExitType_i = k) = f(HistoricalCapacity_i, X_i, \delta_p),
\end{equation}

where $k$ indexes the four institutional fates. In the present version, I
report the simpler institutional-change model because the validated sample has
too few substantive-exit cases for a stable multinomial model.

\input{tables/controlled_lpm_institutional_change}

Table \ref{tab:controlled-lpm-institutional-change} reports the
available-controls models that can be estimated for all historically matched
gold-standard rows without imputing missing fiscal data. The
historical-capacity coefficient remains positive after adding source coverage
and capital or sub-provincial status, and it remains positive after adding
provisional platform-hierarchy controls. With province fixed effects, the
coefficient becomes much smaller and is no longer distinguishable from zero.
This pattern is informative because it identifies the level at which the next
empirical work must operate. Some of the raw capacity pattern is plausibly
absorbed by provincial policy environments and by the geography of platform
finance. The measurement result remains intact, but the adjusted explanatory
claim must be stated cautiously until the validated sample and the
contemporary controls are broader.

\input{tables/full_controls_lpm_institutional_change}

Table \ref{tab:full-controls-lpm-institutional-change} reports the
first-pass full-controls models for the subset of validated cases with
source-backed contemporary controls. The model adds GDP per capita, fiscal
self-sufficiency, statutory debt pressure, a land-finance proxy, source
coverage, and provisional platform hierarchy. The historical-capacity
coefficient is positive in the historical-only model, but it attenuates after
economic, fiscal, debt, land-finance, disclosure-quality, and platform-level
controls are added. In the full first-pass specification, the coefficient is
close to zero.

This attenuation is not a failure of the measurement framework. It is a useful
diagnostic. The current full-controls sample contains seventy-eight rows, but
the outcome remains highly imbalanced: most cases are nominal exits, while
substantive exit and functional transfer remain relatively rare. The source
base is also intentionally conservative. Contemporary controls are collected
from official statistical communiques, budget and final-account reports,
local-government bond disclosures, rating reports, and public-data
compilations when official tables have not yet been located. Missing values
are not imputed. The resulting estimates show what the paper can estimate
today and where the remaining data collection matters most.

The control results also sharpen the substantive interpretation. High
contemporary fiscal capacity is not sufficient for substantive exit. Beijing,
Chengdu, Ningbo nominal-comparison cases, Shaoxing, Huzhou, Suzhou Wuzhong,
and Zhengzhou show that strong economies and high administrative rank can
still coexist with nominal exit when development-zone functions, platform
hierarchy, public-investment mandates, or continuing fiscal support remain in
place. High debt pressure is also not sufficient to produce institutional
change. Zhenjiang, Bengbu, Suzhou in Anhui, Ji'an, Jingdezhen, Fuzhou in
Jiangxi, Yiyang, Harbin, Hengyang, and Dezhou remain nominal exits despite
substantial statutory debt pressure or weak fiscal self-sufficiency. By
contrast, the functional-transfer cases in Hangzhou, Wenzhou, Foshan, Yixing,
Taizhou, and Xiaogan show reorganization rather than disappearance of the
public function. The evidence therefore points to a more precise claim:
capacity and fiscal conditions shape the institutional route through which
official exit is implemented, but the present sample does not yet justify a
strong claim about a stable adjusted historical-capacity coefficient.

\subsection{LLM surrogate labels}

The LLM-assisted screening file expands the measurement process beyond the
gold-standard file. It contains 361 candidate disclosure rows, of which 346
are usable screening observations. Table \ref{tab:llm-screening-status}
separates the broader screening file from the narrower exit-type outcome
sample. The outcome sample contains ninety-four gold-standard labels and 203
LLM surrogate exit-type labels. The remaining usable screening rows include
forty-four source packets for which the model found no direct formal exit or
compliance event and five human-reviewed boundary packets. These rows are
informative for source coverage and attrition, but they are not treated as
exit-type outcomes.

\input{tables/llm_screening_status}

The current surrogate rule is intentionally narrow. It identifies nominal-exit
cases with medium confidence when the source packet pairs formal compliance
language with continuing public project, land development, entrusted
construction, fiscal support, or state-asset functions. It is not a general
classifier for all four exit types. The 203 disclosure-level surrogate labels
correspond to 158 unique issuers because repeated bond disclosures often
describe the same platform. Table \ref{tab:surrogate-empirical-flow} converts
the disclosure-level screening file into the issuer-level object used for
empirical analysis. Sixty-one issuer-level surrogates overlap independently
validated gold-standard cases and are used only as validation checks.
Ninety-seven non-overlap issuers enter the validation-adjusted descriptive
sample as auxiliary labels.

\input{tables/surrogate_empirical_flow}

Table \ref{tab:dsl-surrogate-validation} reports the first validation
diagnostic. All sixty-one overlap issuers match the gold-standard label:
nominal exit. I do not interpret this raw precision of 1.000 as literal
certainty. With a Jeffreys correction, the implied precision is 0.992. Using
the Wilson 95 percent lower bound gives a more conservative rate of 0.941.
Applied to the ninety-seven non-overlap surrogate issuers, these estimates
imply between 91.25 and 96.22 expected nominal-exit cases. The result
validates the current surrogate rule as a high-precision screen for nominal
exit. It does not validate unresolved rows as negative outcomes, and it does
not validate the model as a full classifier for substantive exit, functional
transfer, or liquidation.

\input{tables/dsl_surrogate_validation}

Table \ref{tab:dsl-adjusted-outcomes} reports the corresponding adjusted
outcome distribution. The gold-standard file contains ninety-four cases, of
which eighty-two are nominal exits and twelve are substantive exits or
functional transfers. Adding only the ninety-seven non-overlap issuer-level
surrogates produces a 191-row adjusted descriptive sample. Under the
Jeffreys-smoothed precision estimate, the sample contains 178.22 expected
nominal exits. Under the conservative Wilson lower bound, it contains 173.25
expected nominal exits. Because the current surrogate rule is one-sided, the
non-nominal column should be read as expected classification error or
unobserved institutional change, not as directly observed non-nominal labels.

\input{tables/dsl_adjusted_outcome_distribution}

For inference, I follow the design-based supervised learning logic of
\citet{egami2023dsl}. Let $Y_i$ denote the unobserved gold-standard exit label,
$S_i$ the LLM screening outcome, and $V_i$ an indicator for whether case $i$ is
human validated. In the current implementation, $S_i$ is a one-sided surrogate
for nominal exit. Downstream estimands, including the association between
historical capacity and institutional change, should therefore be estimated
with a validation-adjusted or doubly robust correction rather than with raw
surrogate labels.

The corrected design has three steps. First, draw a validation sample with
known sampling probabilities, including oversamples of low-capacity and
high-debt localities where source ambiguity is likely to be greatest. Second,
estimate the relationship between surrogate labels and gold labels using
source coverage, document type, historical capacity, debt pressure, land
finance dependence, platform characteristics, and disclosure quality. Third,
estimate the substantive relationship using gold labels where available and
predicted or adjusted outcomes where only surrogate labels are available,
propagating uncertainty from the validation design. The LLM is therefore an
auxiliary measurement device. It expands the source review and helps model
labeling error, but it does not replace the human-validated outcome scale.

\subsection{Mechanism evidence}

The mechanism evidence connects the exit labels to the capacities specified in
the theory. Fiscal absorption appears when former platform functions move into
budgetary channels, special-purpose bonds, or fiscally funded project
management. Bureaucratic coordination appears in asset transfers, debt
succession, municipal platform-group reorganization, SASAC entrustment, and
cross-agency implementation documents. Project and state-asset governance
appears when the post-exit arrangement clarifies project preparation,
repayment responsibility, fiscal settlement, asset operation, and the boundary
between state-owned enterprise activity and implicit government financing.

Appendix Table \ref{tab:pilot-mechanism-evidence} reports qualitative
mechanism evidence for the historically matched validated cases. The
substantive-exit cases show evidence that former platform functions were
absorbed into formal fiscal channels or were no longer visible in the reviewed
platform-function categories. The functional-transfer cases show coordination
and reorganization, but the public function remains inside a local
state-owned or platform-like system. The nominal-exit cases tend to preserve
public project, repayment, land-development, entrusted-construction, or fiscal
support functions inside the same issuer after the formal compliance event.

The empirical implication is that the measurement framework is doing more than
renaming official exit. It identifies where the fiscal function goes. In
high-capacity substantive-exit cases, formal exit is paired with fiscal
absorption or credible negative evidence of continued platform financing. In
functional-transfer cases, formal exit is paired with reorganization inside the
local state-owned system. In nominal-exit cases, formal exit is paired with
continued platform-like fiscal functions. This is the institutional variation
that aggregate exit counts obscure.
