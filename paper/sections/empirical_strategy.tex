\section{Empirical Strategy}

\subsection{Main specifications}

The baseline empirical analysis estimates whether historical state capacity
predicts the probability of substantive exit. A simple specification is:

\begin{equation}
SubstantiveExit_i = \alpha + \beta HistoricalCapacity_i + X_i'\gamma + \epsilon_i,
\end{equation}

where $SubstantiveExit_i$ indicates whether city-platform case $i$ achieved
substantive exit, $HistoricalCapacity_i$ measures historically rooted state
capacity in the relevant locality, and $X_i$ includes contemporary fiscal,
economic, debt, and political controls.

The first version of $HistoricalCapacity_i$ will be constructed from CHGIS
\citep{chgis2024}. The main measure is Qing administrative density: the number
of late-imperial county or prefectural administrative units intersecting the
contemporary prefecture-level city, scaled by land area. This measure captures
the depth of historical administrative presence rather than contemporary fiscal
strength. The main alternative measure will use CBDB to construct Ming-Qing
elite density, especially the density of high-degree civil service examination
holders by place of affiliation \citep{cbdb2024,bai2016elite}. The first
operational version counts jinshi and juren records by historical place,
assigns those places to contemporary prefecture-level cities using their
coordinates and modern administrative boundaries, and scales the resulting
counts by prefecture land area. This second measure captures local
administrative human capital and elite formation. A third measure, based on
Ming courier routes and stations, can be used as a robustness check for
historical communication and transport infrastructure.

A second specification uses exit type as a multinomial outcome:

\begin{equation}
Pr(ExitType_i = k) = f(HistoricalCapacity_i, X_i),
\end{equation}

where $k$ indexes substantive exit, nominal exit, functional transfer, and
liquidation. This specification evaluates whether historical state capacity is
associated with different institutional responses to the same central reform.

\subsection{Pilot evidence}

The first empirical step is an expanded pilot sample. The purpose of the pilot
is not to estimate the main effect. The purpose is to show that the exit-type
categories can be assigned from original documents, that the categories
distinguish different institutional outcomes, and that the cases span
meaningful variation in historical state capacity. For this reason, I separate
human-validated labels from candidate cases throughout the analysis.

Table \ref{tab:pilot-validation-tiers} summarizes the validation status of the
current evidence packets. The human-validated label file now contains
eighty-seven cases, and the broader screening file contains 303 disclosure rows.
The descriptive tables in this section use the seventy-seven validated cases that can
currently be matched to the CBDB-GADM historical-capacity crosswalk. Ten
validated cases remain unmatched because the current crosswalk does not yet
provide a usable historical-capacity match for those city or county entries. This
keeps the pilot evidence auditable while making clear that source collection
has moved beyond the original twelve-case demonstration.

Table \ref{tab:pilot-capacity-bin-exit-type} gives the simplest descriptive

low-capacity cases are all nominal exits. The high-capacity cases contain all
three observed types, including the two substantive-exit cases. This is the
pattern implied by the theory: higher-capacity cities are not guaranteed to
produce substantive exit, but substantive exit is more plausible where local
officials can absorb fiscal functions, coordinate reorganizations, and govern

Table \ref{tab:pilot-lpm-institutional-change} reports a small exploratory
linear probability model using the same matched pilot cases. The dependent
variable equals one when a case is coded as substantive exit or functional
transfer and zero when it is coded as nominal exit. This outcome does not treat
functional transfer as equivalent to substantive exit. It simply separates
cases in which the institutional form changes from cases in which the same
issuer continues the public function under formal compliance language. Across
three simple specifications, historical capacity is positively associated with
this broader measure of institutional change. A one-standard-deviation
increase in elite density is associated with a 6.3 percentage point higher
probability of substantive exit or functional transfer. Membership in the high
historical-capacity bin is associated with a 17.1 percentage point higher
probability relative to the other bins. Moving from low to middle to high
capacity is associated with a 13.5 percentage point increase at each step.
These estimates remain descriptive, but they make the pilot evidence easier to
compare with the paper's eventual full-sample analysis.

Case comparisons clarify the mechanism. Guangzhou and Ningbo illustrate
high-capacity substantive exit. Guangzhou shifted away from new government
project investment and financing toward fiscally funded project management and
budgetary debt replacement. Ningbo pairs no-government-financing language with
negative evidence on core platform functions: the reviewed documents do not
show major PPP, BT, government purchase service, land preparation, advance
financing, or fiscal repayment arrangements. Xuzhou, Yancheng, and Hengyang
point in the opposite direction. Their documents contain formal exit or
no-government-financing language, but the same issuers continued to show
shantytown redevelopment, entrusted construction, land preparation, government
settlement, public project repayment, or fiscal subsidy arrangements.

The expanded cases prevent a deterministic reading of the theory. Several
high-capacity or middle-capacity platforms still receive nominal-exit labels.
Nanjing Transportation Construction Investment, Shijiazhuang Transportation
Investment, Haian Tenghai Investment, Gaochun International Cittaslow Cultural
Tourism Investment, and Jiaxing Jiaxiu Development Investment all contain
formal compliance language, yet their source packets continue to show
transport, development-zone, cultural-tourism, resettlement-housing,
land-preparation, entrusted-construction, fiscal-subsidy, or
government-related receivable functions. These cases show why the paper treats
historical capacity as shaping the feasible menu of responses rather than
mechanically producing substantive exit. Substantive exit requires more than
compliant language. Nominal exit can satisfy formal reporting demands while
leaving the fiscal function institutionally present.

Appendix Table \ref{tab:pilot-mechanism-evidence} connects these labels to the
three mechanisms in the theory section. The entries are qualitative evidence
notes rather than numerical scores. They show whether each case contains
evidence of fiscal absorption, bureaucratic coordination, and project or
state-asset governance. The substantive-exit cases display evidence that
former platform functions were either absorbed into fiscal channels or no
longer visible in the reviewed platform-function categories. The nominal-exit
cases tend to preserve public project or repayment functions inside the same
issuer. The functional-transfer cases show more coordination and
reorganization, but the public function remains inside a local state-owned or
platform-like system.

Figure \ref{fig:pilot-exit-type-distribution} reports the exit-type

Appendix Table \ref{tab:pilot-historical-capacity} reports the case-level
CBDB-GADM elite-density match for seventy-seven validated cases and three candidate
cases \citep{cbdb2024,gadm2022}. The descriptive pattern is not a simple

historical capacity changes the feasible menu of institutional responses
rather than mechanically determining one outcome. At the lower end of the
historical-capacity distribution, the validated cases of Zhuzhou, Xuzhou, and
Yancheng are all coded as nominal exit. Their documents contain formal exit or
no-government-financing language, but the same issuers continued to perform
land preparation, major project construction, shantytown redevelopment,
infrastructure construction, entrusted construction, affordable housing, or
fiscal-support functions. Luzhou, Zunyi, and Liupanshui remain candidate cases

localities are central to the next round of data collection. The historical
capacity bins in Table \ref{tab:pilot-capacity-bin-exit-type} sharpen this
pattern: the high-capacity third contains both substantive-exit cases and five
of the matched functional-transfer cases, while the low-capacity third contains
only nominal exits. This is descriptive rather than causal, but it is exactly
the pattern the measurement framework is meant to make visible. The
documentary trail in lower-capacity cases is often dominated by debt-resolution
pressure, fiscal support, and continuing public-project functions rather than
by clean exit-list documents.

The pilot therefore makes a limited but useful empirical contribution. It does
not yet support a final population estimate, and it should not be read as a
representative distribution of LGFV exit types. It does show that the proposed
coding rule separates substantive exit, nominal exit, and functional transfer
in real documents, that these categories vary with a historically rooted
measure of local state capacity, and that the next stage of the project can
scale the same measurement design to the larger LLM-coded candidate pool.

\subsection{Inference with surrogate labels}

The scaled version of the project separates three objects that are often
collapsed in text-as-data applications. The first is the human-validated label,
which is the gold-standard outcome. The second is the Codex or LLM-generated
label, which is an imperfect surrogate for that outcome. The third is the
source-packet status, which records whether a candidate disclosure contains
enough source text to support any label at all. This distinction is important
because measurement error is not likely to be random. A model may overread
formal compliance language as substantive exit, miss continuing public
functions buried in bond documents, or produce no useful label when the
candidate file contains only a disclosure-page title.

The current screening file makes this separation explicit. It contains 303
candidate rows, of which 288 are usable LLM screening observations. Table
\ref{tab:llm-screening-status} separates these rows from the narrower exit-type
outcome sample. The outcome sample contains eighty-seven gold-standard labels
and 131 LLM surrogate exit-type labels. The remaining usable screening rows
include sixty-six source packets for which the model found no direct formal
exit or compliance event and four human-reviewed boundary packets. These
rows are informative for source coverage and screening attrition, but they are
not treated as exit-type outcomes.

The surrogate exit-type labels are medium-confidence nominal-exit cases because
the source packets pair no-government-financing language with continued
evidence of public development, infrastructure construction, entrusted
projects, land development, fiscal support, or state-asset functions. They
correspond to 98 unique issuers because repeated bond disclosures often
describe the same platform. The analysis therefore deduplicates them at the
issuer level before using them as auxiliary labels. Fifty-four of these
issuers match platforms already present in the gold-standard file under
different case IDs. All fifty-four overlap cases have the same gold-standard
label as the surrogate label: nominal exit. This overlap is not counted as new
validation expansion, but it provides a direct estimate of the precision of the
current LLM rule. Forty-four non-overlap issuers remain queued for human
review.

Table \ref{tab:dsl-surrogate-validation} reports the first validation-adjusted
diagnostic. The raw positive predictive value of the surrogate nominal-exit
screen is 1.000 among the fifty-four overlap issuers. I do not treat this as
literal certainty. With a Jeffreys correction, the implied nominal-exit
precision is 0.991; using the Wilson 95 percent lower bound gives a more
conservative precision of 0.934. Applied to the forty-four non-overlap
surrogate issuers, these rates imply between 41.08 and 43.60 expected
nominal-exit cases. The result is substantively useful but narrow. It validates
the current LLM rule as a high-precision screen for nominal exit. It does not
yet validate the model as a full classifier for substantive exit, functional
transfer, or liquidation.

For inference, I follow the design-based supervised learning logic of
\citet{egami2023dsl}. Let $Y_i$ denote the unobserved gold-standard exit label,
$S_i$ the LLM screening outcome, and $V_i$ an indicator for whether case $i$ is
human validated. In the current implementation, $S_i$ should be interpreted as
a one-sided screen. A surrogate nominal-exit label is informative because it
has high validated precision, while a screened no-formal-event row is a
screening result rather than evidence that nominal exit is absent. The next
version will draw a validation sample with
known sampling probabilities and oversample low-capacity and high-debt
localities, where measurement risk is greatest. A prediction model can then
use $S_i$, source coverage, document type, candidate priority, historical
capacity, debt pressure, land finance dependence, and platform characteristics
to estimate the gold-standard outcome. The downstream estimand, such as the
association between historical capacity and substantive exit, is then estimated
with a validation-adjusted or doubly robust correction rather than with raw
surrogate labels.

This procedure changes the role of the model. The LLM is not the final coder,
and its accuracy is not the main quantity of interest. The model helps expand
the candidate pool and provides auxiliary information for prediction. Human
labels identify the outcome scale and estimate the error structure. Cases
without usable source packets enter the workflow as missing-label candidates
rather than as negative outcomes. For the binary specification, the first
corrected estimand is the relationship between historical capacity and a
broader institutional-change outcome that combines substantive exit and
functional transfer. This outcome is more stable in the present gold-standard
sample than a substantive-exit-only model, because there are only two
substantive-exit cases. For the multinomial specification, I estimate
type-specific indicators for substantive exit, nominal exit, functional
transfer, and liquidation only after additional gold labels or validated
surrogate labels recover enough non-nominal cases. In both cases, uncertainty
must reflect the validation design and the fact that the large candidate pool
is measured through imperfect surrogates.

\subsection{Mechanism analyses}

Mechanism analyses examine whether stronger historical state capacity is
associated with greater formal fiscal substitution after exit. Relevant
outcomes correspond to the three capacities in the theory section. Fiscal
absorption can be measured through special-purpose bond use, on-budget
infrastructure spending, and the movement of former platform projects into
budgetary channels. Bureaucratic coordination can be measured through formal
asset transfers, debt-succession arrangements, municipal platform-group
reorganizations, and cross-agency implementation documents. Project and
state-asset governance can be measured through eligible project preparation,
fiscal-settlement rules, reductions in entrusted-construction receivables,
changes in non-platform local SOE debt, and evidence that post-exit firms no
longer recreate implicit government financing. These outcomes are not the main
dependent variables of the paper; instead, they help explain why some
localities are able to achieve substantive exit while others rely on responses
such as nominal exit or functional transfer.
