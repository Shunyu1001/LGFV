# LGFV Exit Type Coding Protocol

This codebook defines the outcome used in the LGFV exit project. The coding
task is to distinguish formal compliance from functional fiscal change. An
official exit statement, market-oriented transformation statement, or
no-government-financing clause is evidence about regulatory status. It is not,
by itself, evidence that the local government financing function has ended.

## 1. Unit of Analysis

The preferred unit is a city-platform case. A case links one platform company
to one prefecture-level city and one formal exit or compliance event.

A platform company includes a local government financing vehicle, city
investment company, urban construction investment company, development
investment company, transportation investment company, state capital operation
company, or similar local state-owned entity that has performed public project
financing, infrastructure construction, land preparation, policy housing,
public utility, debt rollover, or other quasi-fiscal functions.

If multiple companies in the same city are involved in the same reorganization,
code the focal issuer first and record related entities as part of the
post-event function. Do not collapse a city into a single label unless the
research design explicitly uses a city-level aggregation rule.

## 2. Inclusion Rules

Include a case only when all of the following conditions are met:

- The entity is located in mainland China and is tied to a prefecture-level
  city, district, county, development zone, or municipal-level state-owned
  asset system.
- The entity plausibly performed an LGFV or platform-like function before the
  event.
- The case has at least one formal exit, compliance, restructuring,
  market-oriented transformation, list-removal, no-government-financing,
  liquidation, deregistration, or comparable event.
- The source packet contains evidence about the post-event function, or enough
  negative evidence to support the absence of continuing platform functions.
- The source packet identifies the company, city, source type, source date, and
  relevant excerpts or line references.

## 3. Exclusion Rules

Exclude a case from final labeling when any of the following applies:

- The entity is a purely commercial SOE with no evidence of platform-like
  public financing or project functions.
- The entity is a central SOE, national policy bank vehicle, or provincial
  entity without a city-platform role.
- The source packet contains only a corporate profile or news summary with no
  formal event.
- The source packet contains a formal event but no post-event evidence.
- The source packet contains post-event public investment evidence but no
  formal exit, compliance, transformation, or liquidation event.
- The available sources are contradictory and cannot be reconciled through
  dates, issuer scope, related-entity structure, or source hierarchy.

Cases that fail the final-label threshold can still be retained as candidate,
boundary, or source-only cases. They should not enter the human-validated label
file.

## 4. Source Hierarchy

Use original or near-original documents whenever possible.

Primary sources for formal events:

- Government announcements
- Finance-bureau notices
- NDRC documents
- State-owned asset supervision documents
- Banking-regulator or debt-management documents
- Company announcements
- Business registration records
- Court, liquidation, bankruptcy, or deregistration records

Primary sources for post-event functions:

- Bond prospectuses
- Credit rating reports
- Legal opinions
- Annual reports
- Audited financial statements
- Exchange disclosures
- Debt-replacement or debt-resolution announcements
- Municipal budget and special-purpose bond documents
- Company announcements and official project disclosures

Supplementary sources:

- Credible financial news
- Company profiles
- Local government work reports
- Industry databases

Supplementary sources can help locate events and entities. They do not
determine the final label unless they quote or reproduce original documents.

## 5. Core Coding Question

After the formal event, where did the government financing or quasi-fiscal
function go?

Possible answers:

- It ended or moved into formal fiscal channels.
- It remained inside the same platform.
- It moved to another local state-owned or platform-like entity.
- The company disappeared through liquidation or termination.
- The sources are insufficient.

## 6. Exit Type Categories

### 6.1 Substantive Exit

Definition: The platform genuinely ceases to perform government financing or
quasi-fiscal financing functions after the formal event.

Required evidence:

- A formal exit, no-government-financing, transformation, debt replacement, or
  comparable event.
- Post-event evidence that the issuer no longer performs core platform
  financing functions.
- No continuing evidence that the issuer uses its balance sheet for public
  project financing, land preparation financing, entrusted construction
  financing, fiscal repayment, government purchase service, implicit debt
  rollover, or comparable quasi-fiscal activity.

Supporting indicators:

- Former government projects are funded through budgetary channels or
  special-purpose bonds.
- The issuer receives fee-based project management income rather than carrying
  public project financing exposure.
- Prospectuses, rating reports, and legal opinions describe market-oriented
  operations and do not reveal continuing platform functions.
- Debt replacement or fiscal absorption documents show that legacy platform
  obligations moved into formal fiscal management.

Do not require the company to stop all public-sector activity. Public project
management can coexist with substantive exit if the financing function has
been absorbed by formal fiscal channels and the issuer is no longer acting as
an off-budget financing arm.

### 6.2 Nominal Exit

Definition: The platform has formal exit, no-government-financing, or
market-oriented transformation language, but the same issuer continues the
government financing or quasi-fiscal function.

Required evidence:

- A formal exit or compliance event.
- Post-event evidence that the same issuer continues core platform functions.

Supporting indicators:

- Continuing infrastructure investment or financing.
- Entrusted construction, BT, PPP, government purchase service, land
  preparation, shantytown redevelopment, policy housing, public utility, or
  major public project functions.
- Fiscal subsidies, fiscal repayment, project repurchase, government-settlement
  receivables, or government-linked repayment arrangements.
- Continued dependence on government support, asset injections, guarantees,
  debt replacement, or debt-resolution arrangements.
- Legal language that new debt is not local government debt appears alongside
  evidence of continuing public project obligations.

Nominal exit is the correct label when compliance language and continued
function coexist within the same issuer.

### 6.3 Functional Transfer

Definition: The original platform exits or reduces its role, but the relevant
government financing, construction, debt-management, or public project
function moves to another local SOE, merged entity, subsidiary, newly created
platform, or state capital operation structure.

Required evidence:

- A formal exit, reorganization, merger, asset transfer, debt succession,
  platform consolidation, or comparable event involving the original issuer.
- Evidence that the public function continued through another related local
  state-owned or platform-like entity.

Supporting indicators:

- Another local SOE takes over projects, assets, debt instruments, guarantees,
  or public-service functions.
- Government documents describe asset transfer, merger, group consolidation,
  debt succession, or functional reassignment.
- A new urban development, city investment, state capital operation, housing
  security, transportation, or construction investment group assumes similar
  functions.
- The old platform's direct role declines, but the city preserves quasi-fiscal
  financing through a reorganized state-owned system.

Functional transfer is different from nominal exit because the function does
not simply remain inside the same issuer. It is also different from
substantive exit because the function is not absorbed into formal fiscal
channels or terminated.

### 6.4 Liquidation

Definition: The platform is dissolved, deregistered, liquidated, bankrupted,
or otherwise ceases active operations after the formal event.

Required evidence:

- Business registration, court, company, or government records showing
  cancellation, deregistration, liquidation, bankruptcy, dissolution, or
  termination.
- No evidence that the same legal entity continues active public financing or
  platform functions after the termination event.

Supporting indicators:

- The company stops issuing debt.
- The company no longer undertakes projects.
- Remaining assets, liabilities, or personnel are disposed of through a
  documented liquidation or restructuring process.

If the company disappears but the same function is reassigned to another local
state-owned entity, code functional transfer rather than liquidation unless
the research question is explicitly about legal-entity survival.

## 7. Edge Cases

Fee-based project management after exit:

Code substantive exit if the issuer manages government projects for a fee and
the financing is clearly budget-funded or otherwise inside formal fiscal
channels. Code nominal exit if the issuer finances the project, carries
government repayment exposure, or accumulates fiscal receivables tied to the
project.

No-government-financing language in bond documents:

Treat this language as formal compliance evidence. Do not code substantive
exit unless the same packet lacks continuing function evidence or contains
positive evidence that the financing function ended.

Debt replacement:

Debt replacement supports substantive exit only when it moves legacy
obligations into formal fiscal channels and no new quasi-fiscal function is
visible. If debt replacement coexists with continued platform financing, code
nominal exit.

Merged groups and state capital operation companies:

Code functional transfer when the original platform's function moves into a
merged group or state capital operation company that continues public project
financing or debt management. Code substantive exit only if the function is
absorbed into formal fiscal channels rather than another platform-like entity.

Parent and subsidiary disagreement:

Code at the focal issuer level. If the issuer exits but a subsidiary continues
the same function under the same group structure, record the related entity and
consider functional transfer or nominal exit depending on whether the fiscal
function remains effectively within the same issuer group.

Specialized infrastructure SOEs:

Include only if the entity performs platform-like financing or quasi-fiscal
functions. Exclude purely operating companies that run commercial utilities or
transport services without public financing or fiscal repayment exposure.

Capital cities and high-ranking platforms:

Do not infer substantive exit from administrative rank, city wealth, or
disclosure quality. Apply the same formal-event and post-event-function rule.

Liquidation with successor entity:

If the legal entity is liquidated and no successor takes over the fiscal
function, code liquidation. If a successor entity takes over the function, code
functional transfer and record the liquidation event as part of the transfer
process.

## 8. Scores

### 8.1 Confidence Score

The confidence score records how strongly the available evidence supports the
final label.

High:

- Formal event is directly documented.
- Post-event function is directly documented or clearly absent in rich source
  materials.
- The final label is robust to plausible alternative interpretations.

Medium:

- Formal event is documented, but post-event function evidence is partial.
- The final label is plausible, but one alternative label remains credible.
- Source hierarchy is acceptable but not ideal.

Low:

- Evidence is thin, indirect, or source-only.
- Formal event or post-event function is missing.
- Documents conflict in a way that cannot be fully reconciled.

Only high and medium confidence cases should enter the human-validated label
file. Low confidence cases should be retained as candidates or unclear cases.

### 8.2 Source Coverage Score

The source coverage score ranges from 0 to 4.

0:

- No usable original or near-original source.

1:

- One source identifies either the formal event or the post-event function, but
  not both.

2:

- Sources cover both the formal event and the post-event function, but either
  source type, date range, or issuer scope is limited.

3:

- Multiple source types cover both the formal event and the post-event
  function. At least one source is original or near-original.

4:

- Rich source packet with multiple original or near-original documents,
  credible source hierarchy, clear dates, clear issuer scope, and line
  references or excerpts for both formal event and post-event function.

### 8.3 Continued-Function Evidence Score

The continued-function evidence score ranges from 0 to 4. It measures the
strength of evidence that platform-like functions continued after the event.

0:

- No continuing platform function found in rich post-event sources.

1:

- Weak or indirect evidence of public-sector activity, but no clear
  quasi-fiscal financing function.

2:

- One continuing function is documented, such as public project construction,
  land preparation, fiscal receivables, or government support.

3:

- Multiple continuing functions are documented, or one function is tied clearly
  to fiscal repayment, government purchase service, project repurchase, or
  entrusted construction.

4:

- Strong, repeated evidence across source types that the issuer or successor
  entity continues core platform financing, public project financing, fiscal
  repayment, debt rollover, or comparable quasi-fiscal functions.

This score does not mechanically determine the label. For example, a high
continued-function score inside a successor entity may indicate functional
transfer rather than nominal exit.

## 9. Decision Rule

Assign labels in the following order:

1. Confirm that the case meets the inclusion rules.
2. Identify the formal event.
3. Identify the baseline platform function before the event.
4. Identify the post-event location of that function.
5. Apply the exit type definitions.
6. Assign confidence, source coverage, and continued-function evidence scores.
7. Record the most plausible alternative label and why it was rejected.
8. Mark the case as unclear if the formal event or post-event function cannot
   be established.

## 10. Human Validation

LLM labels are preliminary. The final label is assigned only after human
review of the original source packet.

Each final case record should include:

- Case identifier
- City and province
- Company name
- Formal event
- Final label
- Alternative label
- Confidence score
- Source coverage score
- Continued-function evidence score
- Primary source and line references where available
- Secondary source and line references where available
- Human rationale
- Remaining caveat

Human validators should check whether the LLM confused legal compliance
language with functional change, ignored related entities, treated project
management as financing without evidence, or inferred exit from city wealth or
administrative rank.
